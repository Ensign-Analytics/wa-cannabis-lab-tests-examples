Since 9-THCA is a precursor to 9-THC, tracking its levels over time helps us understand product freshness and how far along the decarboxylation process has gone. Think of it as checking your buds potential before the heat hits.

Decarboxylation Chemistry: 9-THCA 9-THC in Infused Mixes

# Filter for Infused Mixes
infused_data <- lab_data_list %>%
  filter(inventory_type_sub_category == "Infused Mixes") %>%
  mutate(month = floor_date(sale_date_month, "month"))

# Summarise average THCA and THC by month
infused_trends <- infused_data %>%
  group_by(month) %>%
  summarise(
    avg_thca = mean(potency_d9thca_perc_avg, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_thc  = mean(potency_d9thc_perc_avg, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  filter(!is.na(avg_thca) & !is.na(avg_thc) & !(avg_thca == 0 & avg_thc == 0)) %>%
  as_tsibble(index = month) %>%
  fill_gaps(avg_thca = 0, avg_thc = 0)

# Convert to long format for ggplot
infused_long <- infused_trends %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = c(avg_thca, avg_thc),
               names_to = "cannabinoid",
               values_to = "percentage") %>%
  mutate(cannabinoid = recode(cannabinoid,
                              avg_thca = "9-THCA (acid form)",
                              avg_thc  = "9-THC (active form)"))

# Plot side-by-side
ggplot(infused_long, aes(x = month, y = percentage, color = cannabinoid)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.7) +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = label_percent(scale = 1)) +
  labs(
    title = "From Precursor to Potency",
    subtitle = "9-THCA  9-THC Over Time in Infused Mixes",
    x = "Month",
    y = "Average Percentage",
    color = "Cannabinoid"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 10) +
  theme(legend.position = "top")

From Acid to Active: THCA to THC Trends in Infused Mixes (Monthly Averages)

Why Does THCA Trend Downward? Heres the story and its got more twist than a pre-roll:

Product shelf life: Over time, cannabis products sitting in warehouses or shelves may degrade or lose potency, especially THCA converting to other forms such as THC, CBN, or degrading entirely.

However, to note, if THCA declines sharply (e.g., from 47% to 24%), and THC increases only slightly, the total cannabinoid mass (THCA + THC) is going down, suggesting loss of total potency, not just a shift from THCA to THC.

Seasonal manufacturing patterns: Early months (e.g., JanApr) likely reflect fresh batches, post-holiday surges, or early-year restocking. Later in the year, you might see smaller or older inventory tested.

Infused Mixes as a category can have lower cannabinoid consistency due to ingredient blends or inconsistent lab homogenization. If more low-THC batches were tested later in the year, average values drop.

Testing or reporting changes: Different labs, changing methods, or shifts in compliance priorities might influence reported averages.

Increased CBD or alternative focus: Some brands shift formulations throughout the year toward less psychoactive content especially in the edible space.

Youre watching decarboxylation in action and market dynamics layered on top. The trend downward from ~47% THCA to ~24% by December is real, and THC follows a similar decline. Call it Potency Seasonality, and yes, we just coined that.
